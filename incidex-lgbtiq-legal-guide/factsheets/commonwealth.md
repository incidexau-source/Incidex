# Know Your Rights: LGBTIQ+ Discrimination & Vilification (Commonwealth)

**Last updated: 31 December 2025**

---

## Overall Rating: 🟡 AMBER

Commonwealth law provides basic protections for sexual orientation, gender identity, and intersex status (added in 2013), but has significant gaps. Very broad religious exemptions allow religious organisations and schools to discriminate against LGBTIQ+ people in most circumstances. There are no vilification protections for LGBTIQ+ people at the federal level.

---

## What's Protected

### Sexual Orientation: ✅ Protected

**What this means:** You cannot be discriminated against because you are gay, lesbian, bisexual, or any other sexual orientation.

**Real-world examples:**
- Your employer cannot fire you for being gay.
- A landlord cannot refuse to rent to you because you're in a same-sex relationship.
- A hospital cannot deny you treatment based on sexual orientation.
- A bank cannot refuse you a loan because you're in a same-sex relationship.

**Limitations:** Protection was added in 2013. However, very broad religious exemptions mean religious organisations can still discriminate in many circumstances.

### Gender Identity: ✅ Protected

**What this means:** You cannot be discriminated against based on your gender identity, including if you are transgender, non-binary, genderfluid, or agender.

**Real-world examples:**
- Your employer must use your correct name and pronouns.
- You cannot be denied employment because you are transitioning.
- A landlord cannot refuse housing because you are trans.
- Schools cannot prevent you from using facilities matching your gender identity.

**Limitations:** Protection was added in 2013. The law's definition doesn't explicitly mention non-binary people, though it may cover them. Very broad religious exemptions mean religious schools can discriminate against trans students and staff.

### Intersex Status: ✅ Protected

**What this means:** You cannot be discriminated against based on physical sex characteristics (chromosomes, hormones, anatomy).

**Real-world examples:**
- You cannot be denied medical care based on being intersex.
- An employer cannot ask invasive questions about your body.
- You cannot be excluded from school activities because of sex characteristics.
- A gym cannot refuse you membership because you are intersex.

**Limitations:** Protection was added in 2013. The Commonwealth has committed to updating the terminology from 'intersex status' to 'sex characteristics' but timing is unclear.

---

## Where You're Protected

### EMPLOYMENT ✅
**What you're protected from:**
- Being rejected for a job because of sexual orientation/gender identity/intersex status.
- Being paid less than colleagues because you're LGBTIQ+.
- Being harassed by coworkers or managers.
- Being forced to hide your identity at work.
- Being fired because you transition or come out.

**Not protected from:**
- Performance-based discipline (if you're underperforming for legitimate work reasons).
- Discrimination by religious organisations (they have broad exemptions).

### EDUCATION ✅
**What you're protected from:**
- Public schools refusing to enroll your child because you're a same-sex couple.
- Public universities discriminating in admissions.
- Teachers in public schools intentionally using wrong pronouns.

**Not protected from:**
- Discrimination by religious schools (they can legally discriminate against LGBTIQ+ students and staff).

### HOUSING ✅
**What you're protected from:**
- Landlords refusing to rent because you're in a same-sex relationship.
- Being evicted because of your gender identity or intersex status.
- Real estate agents refusing to show you properties.

### GOODS & SERVICES ✅
**What you're protected from:**
- Being refused service at a café or restaurant.
- A doctor refusing to treat you.
- Hotels refusing to book rooms for same-sex couples.

**Not protected from:**
- Discrimination by religious organisations providing services (e.g., religious welfare agencies).

---

## Exemptions: When Discrimination May Be Legal

### Religious Organisations – BROAD EXEMPTIONS

**What this means:** Religious organisations have very broad exemptions under Commonwealth law. This means they can discriminate against LGBTIQ+ people in many situations where other businesses cannot.

**They CAN:**
- Religious schools can refuse to hire LGBTIQ+ teachers or staff.
- Religious schools can expel or refuse to enroll LGBTIQ+ students.
- Religious organisations can refuse to provide services to LGBTIQ+ people.
- Religious hospitals and aged care facilities can refuse to hire LGBTIQ+ staff.

**They CANNOT:**
- Commonwealth-funded aged care services cannot refuse to accept an LGBTIQ+ resident (though they can still refuse to hire LGBTIQ+ staff).

**Real example:** A Catholic school can refuse to hire a teacher because they are gay or transgender. However, a Commonwealth-funded aged care home cannot refuse to accept an LGBTIQ+ resident.

**If you face discrimination:** If a religious organisation discriminates against you, the exemption is very broad at the Commonwealth level. However, state and territory laws (like those in VIC, QLD, TAS, ACT, or NT) may provide stronger protections. Seek legal advice to understand your options.

---

## Vilification Laws

**Does the Commonwealth have vilification laws for LGBTIQ+ people?**  
❌ NO

There are no federal laws prohibiting anti-LGBTIQ+ vilification (hate speech or conduct that incites hatred). While some states have these laws, the Commonwealth does not.

---

## How to Report Discrimination

You can lodge a complaint with the **Australian Human Rights Commission (AHRC)**.

- **Website:** [humanrights.gov.au](https://humanrights.gov.au)
- **Phone:** 1300 656 419
- **Time Limit:** You must lodge your complaint within **6 months** of the discrimination happening.

## Key Takeaway

Commonwealth law provides a "safety net" across Australia, but it is often weaker than state laws. It protects you from being fired or refused service in most general businesses, but leaves you vulnerable in religious schools and organisations. There is also no federal protection against hate speech (vilification) for the LGBTIQ+ community.
