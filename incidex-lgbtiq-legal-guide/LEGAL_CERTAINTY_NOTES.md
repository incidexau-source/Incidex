# Legal Certainty Notes: LGBTIQ+ Discrimination & Vilification Laws

**Last updated: 31 December 2025**

This document records ambiguities, conflicts, and areas of legal uncertainty identified during research for the Incidex LGBTIQ+ Legal Guide.

---

## Terminology and Definitions

### 1. "Sexual Orientation" vs Jurisdiction-Specific Terms

**Issue:** Different jurisdictions use different terminology for sexual orientation protections.

- **Commonwealth, Victoria, ACT, NT:** Use "sexual orientation"
- **NSW:** Uses "homosexuality" (defined as "male or female homosexuality")
- **Queensland:** Uses "lawful sexual activity"
- **WA, SA, Tasmania:** Use "sexuality"

**Legal Uncertainty:**
- **NSW's "homosexuality"** may not clearly protect bisexual people. The definition refers to "male or female homosexuality" which is binary language. While courts may interpret this broadly, the statutory text is unclear.
- **Queensland's "lawful sexual activity"** is a behavior-based protection rather than identity-based, which may create different legal interpretations.

**Source:** Comparison of Anti-Discrimination Acts across jurisdictions; Alastair Lawrie analysis.

---

### 2. Non-Binary and Gender Diverse Protections

**Issue:** Whether "gender identity" protections clearly cover non-binary and gender diverse people varies by jurisdiction.

**Clear Protection:**
- **Tasmania:** Definition explicitly states "the gender with which the person identifies" (most inclusive)
- **Victoria:** Updated definition (Sept 2025) more inclusive
- **Queensland:** Expanded definition (April 2024) explicitly includes non-binary
- **ACT, NT, SA:** Definitions use inclusive language

**Unclear or Limited Protection:**
- **NSW:** Uses "transgender" defined with binary language ("opposite sex") - does NOT clearly protect non-binary people
- **WA:** Uses "gender history" defined with binary language ("opposite sex") - does NOT clearly protect non-binary people
- **Commonwealth:** Definition does not use binary language but does not explicitly mention non-binary

**Legal Uncertainty:**
NSW and WA's use of "opposite sex" in definitions creates significant uncertainty about whether non-binary and gender diverse people are protected. While some legal commentators argue courts may interpret these provisions broadly, the statutory text is binary.

**Source:** Statutory definitions in each Act; Alastair Lawrie analysis; Equality Australia resources.

---

### 3. Asexual People

**Issue:** Whether "sexual orientation" protections cover asexual people.

**Clear Protection:**
- **Tasmania:** Explicitly includes "asexuality" in definition of sexual orientation

**Unclear:**
- All other jurisdictions do not explicitly mention asexuality

**Legal Uncertainty:**
Only Tasmania explicitly protects asexual people. In other jurisdictions, it is unclear whether asexuality is covered by "sexual orientation" or similar terms. This has not been tested in courts.

**Source:** Anti-Discrimination Act 1998 (Tas), s 3; comparison with other jurisdictions.

## Religious Exemptions

### 4. South Australia: Student Protections Unclear

**Issue:** Whether LGBTIQ+ students in religious schools are protected in SA.

**Competing Provisions:**
- **Section 37** of the Equal Opportunity Act 1984 (SA) provides protections against discrimination by educational authorities against students on basis of sexuality or gender identity
- **Section 50(1)(c)** provides broad religious exemptions

**Legal Uncertainty:**
It is likely that section 50(1)(c) overrides section 37, meaning LGBTIQ+ students in religious schools are NOT protected in SA. However, this has not been definitively tested in courts. Alastair Lawrie's analysis suggests students are likely not protected despite section 37.

**Source:** Equal Opportunity Act 1984 (SA), s 37, s 50; Alastair Lawrie analysis.

---

### 5. Commonwealth: Aged Care Exemption Scope

**Issue:** Scope of Commonwealth-funded aged care exemption.

**Provision:**
The Sex Discrimination Act 1984 (Cth) prohibits discrimination against LGBTIQ+ people accessing Commonwealth-funded aged care services, but religious organisations can still discriminate against LGBTIQ+ employees in these facilities.

**Legal Uncertainty:**
The exact boundaries of this exemption are unclear:
- Does it apply to all Commonwealth-funded aged care or only certain types?
- What constitutes "accessing" services vs employment?
- Can religious aged care providers discriminate in other ways (e.g., refusing to recognize same-sex partners)?

**Source:** Sex Discrimination Act 1984 (Cth), s 37, s 38; Alastair Lawrie analysis.

## Vilification Protections

### 6. NSW: Complex Vilification Framework

**Issue:** NSW has two different vilification frameworks with different coverage.

**Civil Vilification (Anti-Discrimination Act 1977):**
- Covers "homosexuality" (gay men and lesbians only)
- Covers "transgender" (binary identities only)
- Does NOT cover bisexual, non-binary, or intersex people

**Criminal Incitement (Crimes Act 1900, s 93Z):**
- Covers "sexual orientation" and "gender identity" more broadly
- Appears to cover bisexual, non-binary, and intersex people

**Legal Uncertainty:**
- Why are civil and criminal protections different?
- Does the Crimes Act "sexual orientation" cover bisexual people even though the Anti-Discrimination Act "homosexuality" may not?
- This creates confusion about what protections actually exist

**Source:** Anti-Discrimination Act 1977 (NSW), Part 4C; Crimes Act 1900 (NSW), s 93Z; Alastair Lawrie analysis.

### 7. Northern Territory: October 2025 Amendments

**Issue:** October 2025 amendments to NT Anti-Discrimination Act altered offensive behaviour provisions and re-introduced religious exemption.

**Changes:**
- Amendments passed October 2025 (in force as at 31 Oct 2025)
- Re-introduced a religious discrimination exemption (narrower than previous version)
- Altered prohibition against offensive behaviour (s 20A)

**Legal Uncertainty:**
- Exact scope of new religious exemption unclear
- How the altered s 20A affects vilification protections unclear
- Limited public information about these amendments as at 31 Dec 2025

**Source:** Anti-Discrimination Amendment Act 2025 (NT); Equality Australia commentary; news reports.

## Pending Reforms

### 8. Commonwealth: "Intersex Status" to "Sex Characteristics"

**Issue:** Commonwealth committed to updating terminology but timing unclear.

**Commitment:**
As part of Fair Work Act amendments in late 2022, Commonwealth Government promised to update terminology from "intersex status" to "sex characteristics" in both Sex Discrimination Act and Fair Work Act.

**Status as at 31 Dec 2025:**
- Commitment made but no legislation introduced
- Timing unclear
- Sex Discrimination Act still uses "intersex status"

**Source:** Commonwealth Government statements (2022); Sex Discrimination Act 1984 (Cth) current text.

---

### 9. Western Australia: Law Reform Commission Recommendations

**Issue:** WA Government committed to implementing Law Reform Commission recommendations but no legislation passed.

**Recommendations (August 2022):**
- Add "sex characteristics" as protected attribute
- Update "gender history" definition to be more inclusive
- Narrow religious exemptions (similar to Victoria)
- Add LGBTIQ+ vilification protections

**Status as at 31 Dec 2025:**
- WA Government publicly committed to implementing reforms
- No legislation introduced or passed
- Unclear when/if reforms will proceed

**Legal Uncertainty:**
WA currently has significant gaps in protections (no intersex protection, binary gender history definition, broad religious exemptions, no vilification laws). Reforms were promised but not delivered, leaving uncertainty about future protections.

**Source:** WA Law Reform Commission Report (August 2022); WA Government statements; Equal Opportunity Act 1984 (WA) current text.

---

### 10. Queensland: New Anti-Discrimination Act

**Issue:** Queensland Government committed to new Anti-Discrimination Act but not delivered.

**Commitment:**
Following Queensland Human Rights Commission review (2022), Government committed to introduce new Anti-Discrimination Act before end of 2023.

**Status as at 31 Dec 2025:**
- Commitment not met
- Anti-Discrimination Act 1991 (Qld) still in force (with 2024 amendments)
- Unclear when/if new Act will be introduced

**Source:** Queensland Human Rights Commission review (2022); Queensland Government statements.

## Conflicts Between Sources

### 11. Alastair Lawrie Guide (2017) vs Current Law

**Issue:** Alastair Lawrie's "Quick Guide to Australian LGBTI Anti-Discrimination Laws" was published in July 2017 and has not been comprehensively updated for all 2024-2025 reforms.

**Known Discrepancies:**
- **Queensland:** Lawrie's guide predates April 2024 reforms (sex characteristics, expanded gender identity)
- **Victoria:** Lawrie's guide predates September 2025 gender identity definition update
- **NSW:** Lawrie's guide predates July 2025 gender recognition reforms (though these did not update discrimination law)
- **NT:** Lawrie's guide predates late 2022 reforms (sex characteristics, vilification) and October 2025 amendments

**Resolution:**
Where Lawrie's guide conflicts with current legislation text or official government sources, we have followed the legislation text and official sources. Lawrie's guide has been used as Tier 3 verification only.

**Source:** Comparison of Lawrie guide with current legislation and official sources.

### 13. Limitation: No Case Law Analysis

**Note:** This guide is based on black-letter law (statutory text) and official government interpretations only. It does NOT include analysis of case law or tribunal decisions.

**Implication:**
- How courts and tribunals interpret ambiguous provisions (e.g., whether NSW "homosexuality" covers bisexual people) is not analyzed
- Practical application may differ from statutory text
- Users with specific legal questions should seek legal advice

### 14. Limitation: No Analysis of Exceptions and Defenses

**Note:** This guide focuses on major exemptions (religious, small employer) but does not comprehensively analyze all exceptions and defenses in each Act.

**Examples of exceptions not fully analyzed:**
- Sport exemptions (vary by jurisdiction)
- Insurance and superannuation exemptions
- Genuine occupational requirements
- Positive discrimination provisions
- Temporary exemptions

**Implication:**
Users should consult the full Act text and seek legal advice for specific situations.
